Introduction to the special Cosserat theory of rods

This paragraph gives a very brief overview of the special Cosserat theory of rods, as presented in , that accounts for bending, torsion, extension and shear behaviors of slender beams.4 This theory – which is a director theory of rods – was first introduced by . It gives a larger scope to the basements of the present work – which relies on the Kirchhoff theory of rods – as the last is a special case of this larger theoretical framework. Thus, what is presented in this paragraph could be considered as a reasonable starting point to extend the present work, for instance to take account for shear or large extension, which might be relevant for some engineering problems or form-finding processes.

It has been largely employed in various fields .

Description of the motion for a Cosserat rod. This is a typical longitudinal section of a rectangular beam deformed from a reference configuration (bottom) to an actual configuration (top) at time t. Cross-sections are defined in the reference configuration to be planar surfaces perpendicular to the beam axis (${\barr{\mathcal{S}}}$). A material point ${\barr{{\boldsymbol{p}}}} \in {\barr{\mathcal{S}}}(s)$ is located relatively to the cross-section centroid (${\barr{{\boldsymbol{x}}}}(s)$) thanks to its material coordinates (X1, X2, s). During the motion, this material point reaches a new position p ∈ 𝒮(s). The deformed cross-section 𝒮(s) is no more planar. The material frame is no more aligned with the beam axis (d3 and t are not parallel any more). The actual position is measured from the centroid of the deformed cross-section (x(s)) plus an in-plane component (Xαdα) and a deformation vector (u). If the cross-sections deform in a rigid-body manner, then u is null everywhere.


Description of the motion

The special Cosserat theory of rods consider dynamics of rods. It relies on a precise geometric description (see [fig:cosserat_1]) of rods build upon three vector-valued functions that are time dependent :

  • x, a position vector describing the geometry in space of a specific fiber called the rod axis or centerline. This function describes the rod in its longitudinal dimension. This dimension is of prime importance in the case of slender bodies such as rods as what is intend is to build a reduced theory, namely a 1-dimensional theory. This curve will often be understood as the curve passing through the cross-section centroids along the rod, although this is not mandatory in the theory.

  • d1, d2, two unit vector fields describing the lateral spatiality of the rod and called material directors. These vectors will often be understood as the principal axis of the cross-section, although this is not mandatory in the theory.

Modeling the geometry of the rod in any configuration is not sufficient to build a mechanical model. Indeed, one must know a reference state for the solid as strains measure relative change in geometry and stresses are related to strains through the constitutive relation of the rod material. Thus, the special Cosserat theory of rods consider two configurations :

  • The actual configuration, that is the configuration of the rod at time t during the motion.

  • The reference configuration, that is the configuration of the rod in a specific state where its geometry (possibly curved and twisted) is known and its mechanical state (strains, stresses) under possible loads (dead weight, temperature, wind, snow , prestress, …) and possible boundary conditions is known. In practice, this configuration will often be chosen as a stress-free configuration when the beam is not subject to any loads nor restrains of any kind, although this is not mandatory in the theory.

Thus, the equations governing the motion of a special Cosserat rod will be based on the description of a fully known reference configuration and the description of the actual or deformed configuration of the rod at time t during its motion (see [fig:cosserat_1]). Usually, what is intended is to predict the motion of a particular rod given its reference configuration, material properties, boundary conditions, and loading. In this thesis, the equations of the motion will be integrated to converged as fast as possible to the quasi-static response of the system, as this work only deals with statics of structures. However, it is still possible to use a more convenient and accurate time integrator to compute the motion, if one wants to study the (true) dynamic of a rod and go beyond the knowledge of its static equilibrium.

Hereafter, when ambiguity is possible, symbols referring to the reference configuration will be marked with an overline while symbols referring to the actual configuration will be marked with a subscript in the variable t. Generally, scalar quantities are marked with the subscript t and vector quantities with an overline in order to avoid double subscripts when one will refer to vector components.

Actual configuration

At time t, the actual or deformed configuration of the rod {x, d1, d2} is described by its centerline γt ∈ 𝒞1([0, L]×ℝ3), a regular space curve :
$${\begin{array}{lrcl} \gamma_t(t, \cdot): & [0,{L}] & \longrightarrow & \mathbb{R}^3 \\ & {s} & \longmapsto & {\boldsymbol{x}}(t,{s}) \end{array}}$$
and two perpendicular unit vector fields : 5
$${\begin{array}{lrcl} ({\boldsymbol{d}}_1,{\boldsymbol{d}}_2)(t, \cdot): & [0,{L}] & \longrightarrow & \mathbb{R}^3 \times \mathbb{R}^3 \\ & {s} & \longmapsto & ({\boldsymbol{d}}_1(t,{s}), {\boldsymbol{d}}_2(t,{s})) \; / \; {\boldsymbol{d}}_1(t,{s}) \cdot {\boldsymbol{d}}_2(t,{s}) = 0 \end{array}}$$
In addition, we define a third unit vector field as :
d3 = d1 × d2
Thus, the centerline is framed with the orthonormal moving frame {d1, d2, d3}. The unit vectors di(t, s) are called material directors.

Note that the centerline is parametrized by s chosen to be the arc length parameter of the reference configuration. It may not coincide with the arc length parameter of the actual configuration denoted by st = Ψ(t, s)=Ψt(s) as the rod may suffer elongation. L denotes the length of the centerline in the reference configuration. The actual length of γt is denoted by Lt so that st ∈ [0, Lt].

Finally, a material point p of the body is located relatively to the centerline with the help of the local position vector r such that (see [fig:cosserat_2a,fig:cosserat_2b]) :
$${\boldsymbol{p}}({\barr{{\boldsymbol{r}}}},t) = {\boldsymbol{x}}(s,t) + {\boldsymbol{r}} \bigl( {\boldsymbol{x}}(s,t), {\boldsymbol{d}}_1(s,t) , {\boldsymbol{d}}_2(s,t), {\barr{{\boldsymbol{r}}}}, t \bigr)$$
Note that in the above expression a material point is uniquely identified – in a very generic manner – by its local position in the reference configuration (${\barr{{\boldsymbol{r}}}} = {\barr{{\boldsymbol{p}}}} - {\barr{{\boldsymbol{x}}}}$).

Reference configuration

We now identify a reference configuration of the rod $\{{\barr{{\boldsymbol{x}}}}, {\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_1}}, {\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_2}}\}$ with centerline ${\barr{\gamma}} \in \mathcal{C}^1([0,{L}]\times \mathbb{R}^3)$, a regular space curve. This time, s is the arc length parameter of ${\barr{\gamma}}$, which leads to the important relation between ${\barr{{\boldsymbol{x}}}}$ and the unit tangent vector ${\barr{{\boldsymbol{t}}}}$ of ${\barr{\gamma}}$  :
$$\frac{d {\barr{{\boldsymbol{x}}}}}{ds} = {\barr{{\boldsymbol{t}}}} \quad,\quad \norm{{\barr{{\boldsymbol{t}}}}} = 1$$
In this configuration, we define a cross-section 𝒮(s) as the set of material points lying in a plane perpendicular to ${\barr{\gamma}}$ at position ${\barr{{\boldsymbol{x}}}}(s)$. By definition, it is a planar surface in the reference configuration. However, there is no evidence that this surface will remain planar in any other configuration. Moreover, and only for this configuration, it makes sens to choose the centerline as the curve passing through the cross-section centroids.

Finally, we call material coordinates of point ${\barr{{\boldsymbol{p}}}} \in \mathcal{S}(s)$ the triple (X1, X2, s = X3) such that (see [fig:cosserat_2c,fig:cosserat_2d]) :


$$\begin{aligned} {2} &{\barr{{\boldsymbol{p}}}}({\barr{{\boldsymbol{r}}}}) = {\barr{{\boldsymbol{x}}}}(s) + {\barr{{\boldsymbol{r}}}} \bigl({\barr{ {\boldsymbol{x}}}}(s), {\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_1}}(s) , {\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_2}}(s), X_1, X_2 \bigr) \\ &{\barr{{\boldsymbol{r}}}}(X_1, X_2, s) = X_1 {\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_1}} (s) + X_2 {\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_2}}(s) \end{aligned}$$

We also identify a fiber as the set of material points that share the same cross-section coordinates (X1, X2) all along the rod in the reference configuration.

Time evolution

The evolution in time of the rod is simply given by the velocity of its centerline ($\dot{{\boldsymbol{x}}}$) and the angular velocity or spin vector (ω) of its material directors :


$$\begin{aligned} {2} &\frac{\partial {\boldsymbol{x}}}{\partial t} (s,t) = \dot{{\boldsymbol{x}}} \label{eq:velocity_a} \\[0.5em] &\frac{\partial {\boldsymbol{d}}_k}{\partial t} (s,t) = \dot{{\boldsymbol{d}}_k} = {\boldsymbol{\omega}}(s,t) \times {\boldsymbol{d}}_k(s,t) \label{eq:velocity_b} \end{aligned}$$

From now on, the derivative with respect to time is denoted with an overdot symbol.

Strains

Strains are described with the help of the strains vectors η and 𝜘 – respectively the force strains vector and moment strains vector  :


$$\begin{aligned} {2} &\frac{\partial {\boldsymbol{x}}}{\partial s} (s,t) = {\boldsymbol{x}}' = {\boldsymbol{\eta}}(s,t) \label{eq:strains_a} \\[0.5em] &\frac{\partial {\boldsymbol{d}}_k}{\partial s} (s,t) = {\boldsymbol{d}}'_k = {\boldsymbol{\varkappa}}(s,t) \times {\boldsymbol{d}}_k(s,t) \label{eq:strains_b} \end{aligned}$$

where the derivative with respect to s is denoted with a prime symbol.6 The components of η = ηkdk and 𝜘 = 𝜘kdk expressed in the material frame basis {d1, d2, d3} can be interpreted as the classical engineering strains that lead to the engineering stresses.7,8 In particular η1 = x′⋅(d1 × d2) characterizes the change in volume of the body while η2 and η3 characterize the shear deformations ; 𝜘3 is the material twist of the rod while 𝜘1 and 𝜘2 are the material curvatures of the rod.9

Observe the symmetry of [eq:velocity_a,eq:velocity_b] and [eq:strains_a,eq:strains_b] regarding the parameters s and t :

  • ($\dot{{\boldsymbol{x}}}$, ω) governs the time evolution of the material frame.

  • (x′, 𝜘) governs the spatial evolution of the material frame along the centerline.

Parametrization of the centerline

Recall that because the centerline of the reference configuration is parametrized by arc length, the unit tangent vector in this configuration is given by :
$${\barr{{\boldsymbol{t}}}}(s) = \frac{d {\barr{{\boldsymbol{x}}}}}{d s} ({\barr{s}}) = {\barr{{\boldsymbol{x}}}}'(s) \quad , \quad \norm{{\barr{{\boldsymbol{x}}}}'} = 1$$
In the deformed configuration, the centerline is still parametrized by s which is no more an arc length parameter because the centerline has suffered stretch. Thus, the unit tangent vector in this configuration is given by : 10
$${\boldsymbol{t}}(s,t) = \frac{{{\boldsymbol{x}}}'(s,t)}{\norm{{{\boldsymbol{x}}}'(s,t)}} \quad , \quad \norm{{\boldsymbol{x}}'} = \norm{{\boldsymbol{\eta}}'} \neq 1$$
We introduce ϵ, the extension of the rod which characterizes the local change in length of the rod centerline, defined as :
$$\norm{{\boldsymbol{\eta}}'(s,t)} = \frac{\partial s_t}{\partial s}(s,t) = \Psi'(s,t) = 1 + \epsilon(s,t)$$

Inextensibility

The rod is said to be inextensible if ϵ = 0 everywhere and at all time. In this case, s is a valid arc length parameter for the centerline in every configurations. Later, we will restrict to the case of rods subjected to small extension, that is ϵ(t, s)≪1.

Reparametrization

Although either s and st can be chosen as the third material coordinate to describe a rod, the definition of the material strains are given with respect to s and not st. This is a matter of concern as the constitutive relations – classically of the form M = EIκ, N = ESϵ, Q = GJτ – relies upon material strains. Thus, in theses equations, what takes place is a derivation with respect to s and not to st, which matters if the rod is not required to be inextensible.

Hypothesis

  • the rod is slender

  • cross-section deformations remain small, although rotations may be large

  • cross-section shear-center and centroid are at the same location

  • material and cross-section properties vary slowly along the rod

Description of the motion

To describe the motion of a Kirchhoff rod, we use the framework presented in [sec:cosserat_motion] for Cosserat rods.16 However, we restrict its scope by requiring that transverse shear strains are negligible quantities, which is one of the fundamental assumptions made by Kirchhoff in his theory :


$$\begin{aligned} {3} &\eta_1 &&\simeq 0 \\ &\eta_2 &&\simeq 0 \end{aligned}$$

As a consequence, the material frame remains adapted to the centerline. The rod is not supposed to be strictly inextensible. However, as strains are assumed to be small, the axial strain is supposed to be small itself (ϵ ≪ 1), which translates to :


$$\begin{aligned} {3} &\eta_3(s,t) = 1+ \epsilon(s,t) \\ &{\boldsymbol{d}}_3(s,t) = {\boldsymbol{t}}(s,t) \\ &{\boldsymbol{x}}'(s,t) = (1+\epsilon) {\boldsymbol{t}}(s,t) \end{aligned}$$

Stress-free configuration

We now consider a stress-free configuration of the rod as the reference configuration.17 The rod is described by its centerline ${\barr{\gamma}}$ and its material frame $\{{\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_1}}, {\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_2}}, {\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_3}}\}$. Again, a planar cross-section is defined as the set of material points lying in the plane perpendicular to ${\barr{\gamma}}$ and passing through ${\barr{{\boldsymbol{x}}}}(s)$. The material directors ${\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_1}}$ and ${\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_2}}$ are now chosen to be aligned with the principal axes of inertia of the cross-section.18 Thus, ${\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_3}} = {\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_1}} \times {\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_2}}$ is normal to the plane of the cross-section and adapted to the centerline ($ {\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_3}} = {\barr{{\boldsymbol{t}}}}$). Moreover, the centerline is chosen to be the curve passing through the cross-section centroids and is required to be at least a regular space curve, which means that its tangent is continuously defined.

For a sufficiently slender rod, the position of material point ${\barr{{\boldsymbol{p}}}}$ that belongs to cross-section 𝒮(s) (see [fig:cosserat_1,fig:cosserat_2a,fig:cosserat_2b]) is expressed through its material coordinates (X1, X2, s) as : 19


$$\begin{aligned} {2} &{\barr{{\boldsymbol{p}}}}(X_1, X_2, s) &&= {\barr{{\boldsymbol{x}}}}({s}) + {\barr{{\boldsymbol{r}}}}(X_1, X_2, s) \\ &{\barr{{\boldsymbol{r}}}}(X_1, X_2, s) &&= X_1 {\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_1}}(s) + X_2{\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_2}} (s) \end{aligned}$$

Consequently, for each s in the reference configuration, (X1, X2) is a cartesian coordinate system for the plane 𝒮(s). In this system the local coordinates of the cross-section centroid are (0, 0).

Finally, the cross-section is assumed to be bounded and the planar boundary curve is defined by the implicit equation : fs(X1, X2)=0. It is also required that the shear center and the centroid of the cross-section are at the same location, otherwise one would require a more complex kinematic description of the rod.20

Deformed configuration

We now examine the motion of a Krichhoff rod and we call deformed configuration its actual configuration at time t. In this configuration the rod undergoes internal stresses under body loads, external loads and constrains.

The deformed configuration of the rod at time t is described by its centerline γt, its material frame {d1, d2, d3} and a local displacement field u. The centerline of the rod is deformed into the space curve γt with position vector x :
$${\begin{array}{lrcl} \gamma_t: & [0,{L}] & \longrightarrow & \mathbb{R}^3 \\ & s & \longmapsto & {\boldsymbol{x}}(s,t) \end{array}}$$
A material point ${\barr{{\boldsymbol{p}}}}$ in the reference configuration is transported to position p in the actual configuration so that (see [fig:cosserat_1,fig:cosserat_2c,fig:cosserat_2d]) :


$$\begin{aligned} {2} &{\boldsymbol{p}}(X_1, X_2, s, t) &&= {\boldsymbol{x}}(s,t) + {\boldsymbol{r}}(X_1, X_2, s, t) \\ &{\boldsymbol{r}}(X_1, X_2, s, t) &&= X_1 {\boldsymbol{d}}_1 (s,t) + X_2 {\boldsymbol{d}}_2 (s,t) + {\boldsymbol{u}}(X_1, X_2, s, t) \\ &{\boldsymbol{u}}(X_1, X_2, s, t) &&= u_k(X_1,X_2, s, t) {\boldsymbol{d}}_k (s, t) \end{aligned}$$

Although the cross-section 𝒮(s) is a planar surface in the reference configuration, it deforms to a non-planar surface in the actual configuration since u ≠ 0.21 The components (u1, u2, u3)T of the local displacement field expressed in the material frame basis are required to be small in Kirchhoff’s theory of rods.22 In practice, as explained by  this means that the considered motions must satisfy :
$$\frac{u_k}{h} = O(\alpha) \quad , \quad \frac{\partial u_k}{\partial X_1} = O(\alpha) \quad , \quad \frac{\partial u_k}{\partial X_2} = O(\alpha) \quad , \quad \frac{\partial u_k}{\partial s} = O(\alpha^2)$$
In this theory, the material frame in the reference configuration deforms in a rigid-body manner so that it remains orthonormal and aligned to the principal axes of the cross-section – within an error O(α2).23 Remark that this is different than assuming that cross-sections deform in a rigid-body manner, which is known as the Euler-Bernoulli hypothesis and is equivalent to the special case u = 0.

Reparametrization

This subsection highlights the role played by the change in length of the rod during its motion. It was found that this aspect is often treated partially or with confusion in the literature, although it is of prime importance to understand correctly the influence of axial stretch in the computation of moment strains. Indeed, for an inextensible rod, the notions of geometric curvature and (flexural) material curvatures are somehow the same notions. But this is not the case for extensible rods as explained in [sec:kirchhoff_strains].

The rod is parametrized by s, the arc length parameter of the reference configuration, as the constitutive laws will be expressed relatively to this configuration. But recall once again that s is no more the arc length parameter of the deformed centerline as the rod may have suffer axial extension.24 Kirchhoff’s theory assumes that the material frame remains adapted to the centerline during deformation, or equivalently that transverse shear strains are neglected.25 The extension of the centerline is characterized by ϵ defined such that :


$$\begin{aligned} {2} &{\barr{{\boldsymbol{x}}}}' = {\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_3}} \\ &{\boldsymbol{x}}' = (1 + \epsilon) {\boldsymbol{d}}_3 \end{aligned}$$

However, one can parametrized the deformed centerline by its own arc length parameter, denoted st. Let’s call Lt the length of the deformed centerline and Ψt the 𝒞1 diffeomorphism that maps s onto st (st = Ψt(s)⇔s = Ψt−1(st)). Thus, the centerline is equivalently described by :
$${\begin{array}{lrcl} \gamma_t: & [0,{L_t}] & \longrightarrow & \mathbb{R}^3 \\ & s_t & \longmapsto & {\boldsymbol{x}}(s_t) \end{array}}$$
Because st is the arc length parameter of γt the following relations hold :


$$\begin{aligned} {2} &\frac{\partial {\boldsymbol{x}}}{\partial s_t} &&= {\boldsymbol{d}}_3 \\[0.5em] &\frac{\partial s_t}{\partial s} &&= \eta_3 = 1+ \epsilon \end{aligned}$$

Consequently, one can deduced that the derivation with respect to s is proportional to the derivation with respect to st by a factor 1 + ϵ. This factor has to be taken into account when computing the material curvatures, which are no more equivalents to their geometric counterparts in the deformed configuration. This is detailed in the next section dedicated to the strains vectors.

Strains

This section, introduces the material force and moment strains vectors of a Kirchhoff rod. It shows how they are related – yet distinct if ϵ ≠ 0 – to the geometric curvature of the centerline.

Reference configuration

Since the material frame is orthonormal and adapted to the centerline, its evolution along the undeformed centerline is described thanks to the reference material curvature vector ${\barr{{\boldsymbol{\varkappa}}}}$ defined as :
$${\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_i}}' = {\barr{{\boldsymbol{\varkappa}}}} \times {\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_i}}$$
In the reference configuration, because s is the centerline’s arc length parameter, the strains vector components expressed in the material frame basis take the form : 26


$$\begin{aligned} {4} &{\barr{\varkappa}}_1 &&={\barr{ {\boldsymbol{d}}_3}}' \cdot {\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_2}} &&= {\barr{k}}_1 &&= {\barr{{\boldsymbol{\kappa b}}}} \cdot {\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_1}} \\ &{\barr{\varkappa}}_2 &&= {\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_1}}' \cdot {\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_3}} &&= {\barr{k}}_2 &&= {\barr{{\boldsymbol{\kappa b}}}} \cdot {\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_2}} \\ &{\barr{\varkappa}}_3 &&= {\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_1}}' \cdot {\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_2}} &&= {\barr{\tau}} &&= {\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_1}}' \cdot {\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_2}} \end{aligned}$$

where ${\barr{{\boldsymbol{\kappa b}}}}$ (see [eq:kb]) is the curvature binormal vector of ${\barr{\gamma}}$  :
$${\barr{{\boldsymbol{\kappa b}}}} = {\barr{{\boldsymbol{t}}}} \times \frac{\partial {\barr{{\boldsymbol{t}}}}}{\partial s} = {\barr{{\boldsymbol{t}}}} \times {\barr{{\boldsymbol{t}}}}'$$
${\barr{\varkappa}}_1$ and ${\barr{\varkappa}}_2$ are called the reference material curvatures. ${\barr{\varkappa}}_3$ is called the reference material twist. In this configuration, ${\barr{\varkappa}}_1$ and ${\barr{\varkappa}}_2$ are simply computed as the projection of the curvature binormal vector along ${\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_1}}$ and ${\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_2}}$.

Note the important distinction between the reference material twist (${\barr{\tau}}$) and the torsion of Frenet (τf) of the centerline, as defined in [sec:ff].

Deformed configuration

Since the material frame is orthonormal and adapted to the centerline, it’s evolution along the deformed centerline is described thanks to the actual strains vector 𝜘 defined as :
$$\frac{\partial {\boldsymbol{d}}_k}{\partial s} = {\boldsymbol{d}}'_k = {{\boldsymbol{\varkappa}}} \times {\boldsymbol{d}}_k$$
Note that the strains vector is defined relatively to the arc length s of the reference configuration and not the arc length st of the actual configuration. Thus the strains vector components expressed in the material frame basis are given by :


$$\begin{aligned} {3} &{\varkappa}_1 &&= {\boldsymbol{d}}'_3 \cdot {\boldsymbol{d}}_2 &&= (1+\epsilon) {k_1} = (1+\epsilon)\, {\boldsymbol{\kappa b}} \cdot {\boldsymbol{d}}_1 \\[0.5em] &{\varkappa}_2 &&= {\boldsymbol{d}}'_1 \cdot {\boldsymbol{d}}_3 &&= (1+\epsilon) {k_2} = (1+\epsilon)\, {\boldsymbol{\kappa b}} \cdot {\boldsymbol{d}}_2 \\[0.5em] &{\varkappa}_3 &&= {\boldsymbol{d}}'_1 \cdot {\boldsymbol{d}}_2 &&= (1+\epsilon) {\tau} = (1+\epsilon)\, \frac{\partial {\boldsymbol{d}}_1}{\partial s_t} \cdot {\boldsymbol{d}}_2 \end{aligned}$$

where κb (see [eq:kb]) is the curvature binormal vector of γt given by :
$${\boldsymbol{\kappa b}} = {\boldsymbol{t}} \times \frac{\partial {\boldsymbol{t}}}{\partial s_t} = (1+\epsilon)\, {\boldsymbol{t}} \times {\boldsymbol{t}}'$$
𝜘1 and 𝜘2 are called the material curvatures. 𝜘3 is called the material twist. Note this time the dependence of the material strains components (𝜘1, 𝜘2, 𝜘3)T regarding the extension of the rod. These are the strains employed in the classical constitutive laws that lead to the determination of the internal axial force (N = ESϵ), internal bending moments ($M_1 = EI_1(\varkappa_1-{\barr{\varkappa}}_1)$, $M_2 = EI_2(\varkappa_2-{\barr{\varkappa}}_2)$) and internal twisting moment ($Q = GJ((\varkappa_3-{\barr{\varkappa}}_3)$).

Often in the literature the flexural material curvatures are computed as the projection of the curvature binormal vector onto the first two material axes. Here it is demonstrated that this is not exact as it omits the contribution of the rod extension, although it could be a reasonable approximation when ϵ ≪ 1.

Balance of momentum

Internal forces (F) and moments (M) acting on an infinitesimal beam slice of length ds. The beam is also subject to distributed external forces (f) and moments (m).

By convention, internal forces and moments are forces and moments applied by the right part to the left part of the beam. [fig:5_0]

Let 𝒫 be the first Piola-Kirchhoff stress tensor. 𝒫 expresses how contact forces are acting in a deformed body, referring to its (known) reference configuration. Let dS = ndS be an elementary oriented surface of the rod in the reference configuration, of centroid p(X1, X2, s, t)∈𝒮(s).27 The contact forces exerted on dS are given by :


$$\begin{aligned} {2} &{\boldsymbol{dF}}(X_1,X_2,s,t) &&= {\boldsymbol{\sigma_n}}(X_1,X_2,s,t) \,dS \\ &{\boldsymbol{\sigma_n}}(X_1,X_2,s,t) &&= {\boldsymbol{\mathcal{P}}}(X_1,X_2,s,t) \cdot {\boldsymbol{n}} \label{eq:piola} \end{aligned}$$

The Piola stress vector (σn) introduced in [eq:piola] expresses the contact forces exerted on the body per unit area of the reference configuration.28

The generic laws for the balance of linear and angular momentums are obtained by summation over the reference configuration, where b are the body forces per unit volume of the reference configuration :


$$\begin{aligned} {2} &\iiint_{\mathcal{V}} \rho \ddot{{\boldsymbol{p}}} \;dV &&= \iint_{\partial\mathcal{V}} {\boldsymbol{\sigma_n}} \;dS + \iiint_{\mathcal{V}} \rho {\boldsymbol{b}} \;dV \label{eq:linearbalance} \\[0.5em] &\iiint_{\mathcal{V}} \rho ({\boldsymbol{p}} \times \ddot{{\boldsymbol{p}}}) \;dV &&= \iint_{\partial\mathcal{V}} {\boldsymbol{p}} \times {\boldsymbol{\sigma_n}} \;dS + \iiint_{\mathcal{V}} \rho ({\boldsymbol{p}} \times {\boldsymbol{b}}) \;dV \label{eq:angularbalance} \end{aligned}$$

Here, and subsequently, 𝒱 denotes the volume of a slice of the rod in the reference configuration, encompassed between two cross-sections (𝒮1 = 𝒮(s1), 𝒮2 = 𝒮(s2), s1 < s2). We also denote ℒ12 the lateral surface of the rod in the reference configuration so that the exterior surface of the volume is : ∂𝒱 = 𝒮1⋃ℒ12⋃𝒮2.

The cross-section 𝒮(s) splits the rod in two parts. Hereafter, the upstream part of the rod over [s, L] will be called the . Reciprocally, the downstream part of the rod over [0, s] will be called the .

Internal forces and moments

At the cross-section 𝒮(s), the contact forces applied by the right part onto the left part of the rod yield the following resultant force F and resultant moment M about the centroid point x(s, t) :


$$\begin{aligned} {2} &{\boldsymbol{F}}(s, t) &&= \iint_{\mathcal{S}(s)} {\boldsymbol{\sigma_n}}(X_1, X_2, s, t) \;dX_1 dX_2 \label{eq:F} \\[0.5em] &{\boldsymbol{M}}(s, t) &&= \iint_{\mathcal{S}(s)} {\boldsymbol{r}}(X_1,X_2,s,t) \times {\boldsymbol{\sigma_n}}(X_1, X_2, s, t) \;dX_1 dX_2 \label{eq:M} \end{aligned}$$

F and M are commonly known as the internal forces and the internal moments of the rod.

External forces and moments

We assume that the resultant of the contact forces on ℒ12 and the body forces on 𝒱 reduce to the following forms :


$$\begin{aligned} {5} &\iint_{\mathcal{L}_{12}} {\boldsymbol{\sigma_n}} \;dS &&+ \iiint_{\mathcal{V}} \rho {\boldsymbol{b}} \;dV &&= \int_{s_1}^{s_2} {\boldsymbol{f}}_s + (1+\epsilon) {\boldsymbol{f}}_{s_t} \;ds \label{eq:f} \\[0.5em] &\iint_{\mathcal{L}_{12}} {\boldsymbol{p}} \times {\boldsymbol{\sigma_n}} \;dS &&+ \iiint_{\mathcal{V}} \rho ({\boldsymbol{p}} \times {\boldsymbol{b}}) \;dV &&= \int_{s_1}^{s_2} {\boldsymbol{m}}_{s} + (1+\epsilon) {\boldsymbol{m}}_{s_t} \nonumber \\ & && &&\quad + {\boldsymbol{x}} \times \big({\boldsymbol{f}}_{s} + (1+\epsilon) {\boldsymbol{f}}_{s_t} \big) \;ds \label{eq:m} \end{aligned}$$

where fs (resp. fst) is the distributed resultant force per unit length of the reference (resp. deformed) configuration ; and ms (resp. mst) is the distributed resultant moment per unit length of the reference (resp. deformed) configuration. For instance, these distributed forces and moments include external and body loads such as weight, snow, wind, …29

Note that Kirchhoff’s theory require that the stress components on the sides of the rod are small  – that is σn ⋅ n = O(α2) over ℒ12. Thus, the first two terms in the above expression will be neglected :


$$\begin{aligned} {2} &\iint_{\mathcal{L}_{12}} {\boldsymbol{\sigma_n}} \;dS \simeq 0 \\[0.5em] &\iint_{\mathcal{L}_{12}} {\boldsymbol{p}} \times {\boldsymbol{\sigma_n}} \;dS \simeq 0 \end{aligned}$$

Although the continuous model does not account formally for punctual loads,30 they will be introduced seamlessly in the discrete model as the dynamical equations for the motion of the rod will translate into rigid body equations for the discrete segments composing the rod.

Inertial forces

The inertial forces for a volume of the rod encompassed between cross-sections 𝒮1 and 𝒮2 are obtained by summation as :


$$\begin{aligned} {2} &\iiint_{\mathcal{V}} {\rho} \ddot{{\boldsymbol{p}}} \;dV = \iiint_{\mathcal{V}_t} {\rho_t} \ddot{{\boldsymbol{p}}} \;dV_t \label{eq:inertial_a} \\[0.5em] &\iiint_{\mathcal{V}} \rho ({\boldsymbol{p}} \times \ddot{{\boldsymbol{p}}}) \;dV= \iiint_{\mathcal{V}_t} {\rho_t} ({\boldsymbol{p}} \times \ddot{{\boldsymbol{p}}}) \;dV_t \label{eq:inertial_b} \end{aligned}$$

Here, ρ (resp. ρt) is the mass density of the rod in the reference (resp. deformed) configuration. Expressions are given in both coordinate systems.31

In the context of Kirchhoff’s approximation, the local deformations of the cross-sections can be neglected in the computation of the inertial forces . This yields :


$$\begin{aligned} {2} &{\boldsymbol{p}} &&\simeq {\boldsymbol{x}} + X_1{\boldsymbol{d}}_1 + X_2{\boldsymbol{d}}_2 \\ &\dot{{\boldsymbol{p}}} &&= \dot{{\boldsymbol{x}}} + {\boldsymbol{\omega}} \times (X_1{\boldsymbol{d}}_1 + X_2{\boldsymbol{d}}_2) \\ &\ddot{{\boldsymbol{p}}} &&= \ddot{{\boldsymbol{x}}} + \dot{{\boldsymbol{\omega}}} \times (X_1{\boldsymbol{d}}_1 + X_2{\boldsymbol{d}}_2) + {\boldsymbol{\omega}} \times ( {\boldsymbol{\omega}} \times (X_1{\boldsymbol{d}}_1 + X_2{\boldsymbol{d}}_2)) \end{aligned}$$

Since X1 and X2 are the coordinates with respect to the centroid (x) and the principal axes of the cross-section (d1, d2), the cross-section area (S) and principal moments of inertia (I1, I2) are given by : 32,33

[eq:sectionprop]
$$\begin{aligned} {2} &0 &&= \iint_{\mathcal{S}(s)} \left(X_1 X_2\right) \;dX_1 dX_2 \label{eq:diag} \\ &S &&= \iint_{\mathcal{S}(s)} dX_1 dX_2 \\ &I_1 &&= \iint_{\mathcal{S}(s)} {X_2}^2 \;dX_1 dX_2 \\ &I_2 &&= \iint_{\mathcal{S}(s)} {X_1}^2 \;dX_1 dX_2 \end{aligned}$$

More over, for a given cross-section the definition of the centroid yields :

[eq:centroid]
$$\begin{aligned} {2} &{\boldsymbol{0}} &&= \iint_{\mathcal{S}(s)} \left(X_1{\boldsymbol{d}}_1 + X_2{\boldsymbol{d}}_2\right) \;dX_1 dX_2 \label{eq:centroid_a} \\ &0 &&= \iint_{\mathcal{S}(s)} {X_1} \;dX_1 dX_2 \label{eq:centroid_b} \\ &0 &&= \iint_{\mathcal{S}(s)} {X_2} \;dX_1 dX_2 \label{eq:centroid_c} \end{aligned}$$

For a thin slice of the rod (δ𝒱) between cross-sections 𝒮(s) and 𝒮(s + ds), [eq:inertial_a,eq:inertial_b] yield respectively : 34


$$\begin{aligned} {2} &\iiint_{\delta\mathcal{V}} {\rho} \ddot{{\boldsymbol{p}}} \;dV= ({\rho}S \ddot{{\boldsymbol{x}}})ds \\[0.5em] &\iiint_{\delta\mathcal{V}} \rho ({\boldsymbol{p}} \times \ddot{{\boldsymbol{p}}}) \;dV= \left({\rho}S \ddot{{\boldsymbol{x}}} + {\rho} \iint_{\mathcal{S}(s)} {\boldsymbol{r}} \times \ddot{{\boldsymbol{r}}} \; dX_1 dX_2 \right)ds \end{aligned}$$

Finally, remark that :
$${\boldsymbol{r}} \times \ddot{{\boldsymbol{r}}} = (X_1)^2 {\boldsymbol{d}}_1 \times \ddot{{\boldsymbol{d}}_1} + (X_2)^2 {\boldsymbol{d}}_2 \times \ddot{{\boldsymbol{d}}_2} + X_1 X_2 ({\boldsymbol{d}}_1 \times \ddot{{\boldsymbol{d}}_2} + {\boldsymbol{d}}_2 \times \ddot{{\boldsymbol{d}}_1})$$
Thus, reminding [eq:sectionprop,eq:centroid], one can conclude that the inertial forces reduce to :


$$\begin{aligned} {2} &\iiint_{\delta\mathcal{V}} {\rho} \ddot{{\boldsymbol{x}}} \;dV= ({\rho}S \ddot{{\boldsymbol{x}}})ds \label{eq:linearinertia} \\[0.5em] &\iiint_{\delta\mathcal{V}} \rho ({\boldsymbol{p}} \times \ddot{{\boldsymbol{p}}}) \;dV = ({\rho}S \ddot{{\boldsymbol{x}}} + \rho I_1 {\boldsymbol{d}}_1 \times \ddot{{\boldsymbol{d}}_1} + \rho I_2 {\boldsymbol{d}}_2 \times \ddot{{\boldsymbol{d}}_2}) ds \label{eq:angularinertia} \end{aligned}$$

Balance of linear momentum

For a thin slice of the rod (δ𝒱) between cross-sections 𝒮(s) and 𝒮(s + ds), using [eq:F,eq:f], the balance of linear momentum referring to the reference configuration expressed in [eq:linearbalance] yields :
$$\begin{aligned} \iiint_{\delta\mathcal{V}} \rho \ddot{{\boldsymbol{p}}} \;dV &= \iint_{\partial\mathcal{V}} {\boldsymbol{\sigma_n}} \;dS + \iiint_{\delta\mathcal{V}} \rho {\boldsymbol{b}} \;dV \\[0.5em] &= \iint_{\mathcal{S}(s)} {\boldsymbol{\sigma_n}} \;dS + \iint_{\mathcal{S}(s+ds)} {\boldsymbol{\sigma_n}} \;dS + \left( \iint_{\delta\mathcal{L}} {\boldsymbol{\sigma_n}} \;dS + \iiint_{\delta\mathcal{V}} \rho {\boldsymbol{b}} \;dV \right) \\[0.5em] &= -{\boldsymbol{F}}(s) + {\boldsymbol{F}}(s+ds) + \big({\boldsymbol{f}}_{s} (s) + (1+\epsilon) {\boldsymbol{f}}_{s_t}(s) \big) ds \\[0.5em] &= \left( \frac{\partial {\boldsymbol{F}}}{\partial s} + {\boldsymbol{f}}_{s} + (1+\epsilon) {\boldsymbol{f}}_{s_t} \right)(s) ds \end{aligned}$$
Thus, using [eq:linearinertia], the equation for the balance of linear momentum reduce to either equations :


$$\begin{aligned} {1} \frac{\partial {\boldsymbol{F}}}{\partial s} + {\boldsymbol{f}}_{s} + (1+\epsilon){\boldsymbol{f}}_{s_t} &= \rho S \ddot{{\boldsymbol{x}}} \label{eq:linearmotion} \\[0.5em] (1+\epsilon)\frac{\partial {\boldsymbol{F}}}{\partial s_t} + {\boldsymbol{f}}_{s} + (1+\epsilon){\boldsymbol{f}}_{s_t} &= \rho S \ddot{{\boldsymbol{x}}} \end{aligned}$$

Balance of angular momentum

Similarly, for a thin slice of the rod (δ𝒱) between cross-sections 𝒮(s) and 𝒮(s + ds), using [eq:F,eq:M] yields :
$$\begin{aligned} \iint_{\mathcal{S}(s) \bigcup \mathcal{S}(s+ds)} {\boldsymbol{p}} \times {\boldsymbol{\sigma_n}} \;dS &= \iint_{\mathcal{S}(s) \bigcup \mathcal{S}(s+ds)} ({\boldsymbol{x}} + {\boldsymbol{r}}) \times {\boldsymbol{\sigma_n}} \;dS \\[0.5em] &= -({\boldsymbol{x}}\times{\boldsymbol{F}})(s) + ({\boldsymbol{x}}\times{\boldsymbol{F}})(s+ds) - {\boldsymbol{M}}(s) + {\boldsymbol{M}}(s+ds) \\[0.5em] &= \frac{\partial}{\partial s} \big( {\boldsymbol{M}} + {\boldsymbol{x}}\times{\boldsymbol{F}} \big)(s) ds \end{aligned}$$
Using [eq:m] the balance of linear momentum referring to the reference configuration expressed in [eq:angularbalance] yields :


$$\begin{aligned} {5} \label{eq:angularbalance2} &\iiint_{\delta\mathcal{V}} \rho ({\boldsymbol{p}} \times \ddot{{\boldsymbol{p}}}) \;dV &&= \iint_{\partial\delta\mathcal{V}} {\boldsymbol{p}} \times {\boldsymbol{\sigma_n}} \;dS + \iiint_{\delta\mathcal{V}} \rho ({\boldsymbol{p}} \times {\boldsymbol{b}}) \;dV \\[0.5em] & &&= \frac{\partial}{\partial s} \big( {\boldsymbol{M}} + {\boldsymbol{x}}\times{\boldsymbol{F}} \big)(s) ds + {\boldsymbol{m}}_{s} + (1+\epsilon){\boldsymbol{m}}_{s_t} \\ & &&\quad + {\boldsymbol{x}} \times \big({\boldsymbol{f}}_{s} + (1+\epsilon){\boldsymbol{f}}_{s_t} \big) \;ds \end{aligned}$$

Finally, combining [eq:angularbalance2] with [eq:linearmotion,eq:angularinertia], the equation for the balance of angular momentum reduce to either equations : 35


$$\begin{aligned} {1} \frac{\partial {\boldsymbol{M}}}{\partial s} + \frac{\partial {\boldsymbol{x}}}{\partial s} \times {\boldsymbol{F}} + {\boldsymbol{m}}_{s} + (1+\epsilon){\boldsymbol{m}}_{s_t} &= \rho I_1 {\boldsymbol{d}}_1 \times \ddot{{\boldsymbol{d}}_1} + \rho I_2 {\boldsymbol{d}}_2 \times \ddot{{\boldsymbol{d}}_2} \\[0.5em] (1+\epsilon)\Bigl(\frac{\partial {\boldsymbol{M}}}{\partial s_t} + \frac{\partial {\boldsymbol{x}}}{\partial s_t} \times {\boldsymbol{F}} + (1+\epsilon)^{-1}{\boldsymbol{m}}_{s} + {\boldsymbol{m}}_{s_t} \Bigr) &= \rho I_1 {\boldsymbol{d}}_1 \times \ddot{{\boldsymbol{d}}_1} + \rho I_2 {\boldsymbol{d}}_2 \times \ddot{{\boldsymbol{d}}_2} \end{aligned}$$

Equations of motion

With some scaling arguments  shows that terms in ω1 and ω2 should be negligible in the inertial forces of the rod given in [eq:angularinertia], which yields to : 36,37


$$\begin{aligned} {1} \rho I_1 (\dot{\omega}_1 + \omega_2 \omega_3) &\simeq 0 \\ \rho I_2 (\dot{\omega}_2 - \omega_1 \omega_3) &\simeq 0 \\ \rho (I_1 + I_2)\dot{\omega}_3 +\rho(I_2 - I_1)\omega_1 \omega_2 &\simeq \rho (I_1 + I_2)\dot{\omega}_3 \end{aligned}$$

For our application – a beam model for quasi-static analysis of gridshell structures – this approximation is clearly sufficient as what matters is the quasi-static response of the structural system and there is no need for a too accurate modeling of the transient phase. More over, the quasi-static response will be determined through a fictitious dynamic process appropriately damped to speed up the convergence to the steady state, and so there is no reason that the transient phase has any real physical meaning. This means that its is enough to keep only the twisting dynamic of the rod around its centerline.

Thus, the final dynamical equations for the motion of the rod to be retained are :


$$\begin{aligned} {1} \frac{\partial {\boldsymbol{F}}}{\partial s} + {\boldsymbol{f}}_{s} + (1+\epsilon) {\boldsymbol{f}}_{s_t} &= \rho S \ddot{{\boldsymbol{x}}} \\[0.5em] \frac{\partial {\boldsymbol{M}}}{\partial s} + \frac{\partial {\boldsymbol{x}}}{\partial s} \times {\boldsymbol{F}} + {\boldsymbol{m}}_{s} + (1+\epsilon) {\boldsymbol{m}}_{s_t} &\simeq \rho (I_1 + I_2)\dot{\omega}_3 {\boldsymbol{d}}_3 \end{aligned}$$

Hookean elasticity

From now on we consider that the rod material is isotropic and linear elastic.38 This is the framework of the so called Hookean Elasticity. This assumption allows the determination of the local displacement field (u), the strain tensor (ℰ), the stress tensor (𝒮) and the constitutive equations that link the axial force (F3), the bending moments (M1, M2) and the twisting moment (M3) to the strains (ϵ, 𝜘, ${\barr{{\boldsymbol{\varkappa}}}}$).

Such a material is characterized by a linear relation between the strain and stress tensors that takes the form : 39
𝒮 = 2μℰ + λTr(ℰ)ℐ
where λ and μ are known as the elastic coefficients of Lamé. This coefficients are related to the elastic (E) and shear (G) modulus and to the Poisson ratio (ν) :


$$\begin{aligned} {1} \mu &= \frac{E}{2(1+\nu)} = G \\[0.5em] \lambda &= \frac{2\mu\nu}{1-2\nu} \end{aligned}$$

Deformation of cross-sections

Typical deformation modes of cross-sections in Kirchhoff’s theory. Flexion around d1 (resp. d2) is measured through the material curvature 𝜘1 (resp. 𝜘2) ; torsion around d3 is measured through the material twist 𝜘3 ; and ϵ measures the axial extension. Remark that cross-sections are subjected to both in-plane deformations (𝜘1, 𝜘2, ϵ) and out-of-plane deformations (𝜘3).

In this paragraph, we simply recall the canonical form of the local displacement field (u) for the cross-section 𝒮(s) in the context of Kirchhoff’s approximation : 40


$$\begin{aligned} {1} u_1 &= -\nu \epsilon X_1 - \nu(\varkappa_1 - {\barr{\varkappa}}_1) X_1 X_2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\nu(\varkappa_2 - {\barr{\varkappa}}_2)({X_1}^2 - {X_2}^2) \\[0.5em] u_2 &= -\nu \epsilon X_2 + \nu(\varkappa_2 - {\barr{\varkappa}}_2) X_1 X_2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\nu(\varkappa_1 - {\barr{\varkappa}}_1)({X_1}^2 - {X_2}^2) \\[0.5em] u_3 &= (\varkappa_3 - {\barr{\varkappa}}_3)\varphi_s(X_1,X_2) \end{aligned}$$

where φs is the warping function (in torsion) of 𝒮(s), determined by the following differential equation and the boundary condition over the contour of the cross-section : 41


$$\begin{aligned} {5} 0 &= \frac{\partial^2 \varphi_s}{\partial {X_1}^2} + \frac{\partial^2 \varphi_s}{\partial {X_2}^2} && \quad, \quad \forall (X_1,X_2)\in\mathcal{S}(s) \\[0.5em] 0 &= \frac{\partial f_s}{\partial {X_1}}\left(\frac{\partial \varphi_s}{\partial {X_1}} - X_2 \right) + \frac{\partial f_s}{\partial {X_2}}\left(\frac{\partial \varphi_s}{\partial {X_2}} + X_1 \right) && \quad, \quad f_s(X_1,X_2) = 0 \end{aligned}$$

These equations have known analytical solutions for classical shapes such as circles, ellipses, squares or rectangles. For other shapes, when it is not easy to find analytical solutions, the membrane analogy introduced by Prandtl  can be employed.42

Strain tensor

In this paragraph, we simply recall the canonical form of the strain tensor (ℰ) for the cross-section 𝒮(s) in the context of Kirchhoff’s approximation :


$$\begin{aligned} {1} \epsilon_{33} &= \epsilon + (\varkappa_1 - {\barr{\varkappa}}_1) X_2 - (\varkappa_2 - {\barr{\varkappa}}_2) X_1 \label{eq:strain_a} \\ \epsilon_{11} &= \epsilon_{22} = -\nu \epsilon_{33} \label{eq:strain_b} \\ \epsilon_{12} &= 0 \label{eq:strain_c} \\ \epsilon_{31} &= \tfrac{1}{2}(\varkappa_3 - {\barr{\varkappa}}_3)\left(\frac{\partial \varphi_s}{\partial {X_1}} - X_2 \right) \label{eq:strain_d} \\ \epsilon_{32} &= \tfrac{1}{2}(\varkappa_3 - {\barr{\varkappa}}_3)\left(\frac{\partial \varphi_s}{\partial {X_2}} + X_1 \right) \label{eq:strain_e} \end{aligned}$$

Stress tensor

In this paragraph, we simply recall the canonical form of the strain tensor (ℰ) for the cross-section 𝒮(s) in the context of Kirchhoff’s approximation :


$$\begin{aligned} {1} \sigma_{33} &= E \epsilon_{33} \label{eq:stress_a} \\ \sigma_{11} &= \sigma_{22} = \sigma_{12} = 0 \label{eq:stress_b} \\ \sigma_{31} &= 2G \epsilon_{31} \label{eq:stress_c} \\ \sigma_{32} &= 2G \epsilon_{32} \label{eq:stress_d} \end{aligned}$$

Thus, the Piola stress vector defined in [eq:piola] becomes :
σn = σ31d1 + σ32d2 + σ33d3

Constitutive equations for internal forces and moments

In Kirchhoff’s theory, constitutive equations for internal forces and moments should not be considered as assumptions. Indeed, as shown hereafter, they are somehow consequences of the assumptions made on the motion – that is the rod remains close to a motion where cross-sections remain planar, undistorted and perpendicular to the centerline – and on the material – the Hookean elasticity – of the rod.

From [eq:F,eq:piola2,eq:stress_a,eq:strain_a] we deduce the constitutive equation for the axial component of the internal forces : 43
$$ \begin{aligned} F_3 &= \iint_{\mathcal{S}(s)} {\boldsymbol{\sigma_n}}(X_1, X_2, s, t) \cdot {\boldsymbol{d}}_3 \;dX_1 dX_2 \\[0.5em] &= ES\epsilon - (\varkappa_2 - {\barr{\varkappa}}_2) \iint_{\mathcal{S}(s)} {X_1} \;dX_1 dX_2 + (\varkappa_1 - {\barr{\varkappa}}_1) \iint_{\mathcal{S}(s)} {X_2} \;dX_1 dX_2 \\[0.5em] &= ES\epsilon \end{aligned}$$
From [eq:M,eq:piola2,eq:stress_c,eq:stress_d,eq:strain_e,eq:strain_d] we deduce the constitutive equation for the axial component of the internal moments :
$$ \begin{aligned} M_3 &= \iint_{\mathcal{S}(s)} ({\boldsymbol{r}} \times {\boldsymbol{\sigma_n}}(X_1, X_2, s, t)) \cdot {\boldsymbol{d}}_3 \;dX_1 dX_2 \\[0.5em] &= \iint_{\mathcal{S}(s)} -X_2\sigma_{31} + X_1\sigma_{32} \;dX_1 dX_2 \\[0.5em] &= G (\varkappa_3 - {\barr{\varkappa}}_3) \iint_{\mathcal{S}(s)} X_1 \left( \frac{\partial \varphi_s}{\partial {X_2}} + X_1 \right ) - X_2 \left( \frac{\partial \varphi_s}{\partial {X_1}} - X_2 \right ) \;dX_1 dX_2 \end{aligned}$$
From [eq:M,eq:piola2,eq:stress_a,eq:strain_a] we deduce the constitutive equation for the first component of the internal moments :
$$ \begin{aligned} M_1 &= \iint_{\mathcal{S}(s)} ({\boldsymbol{r}} \times {\boldsymbol{\sigma_n}}(X_1, X_2, s, t)) \cdot {\boldsymbol{d}}_1 \;dX_1 dX_2 \\[0.5em] &= \iint_{\mathcal{S}(s)} X_2\sigma_{33} \;dX_1 dX_2 \\[0.5em] &= E (\varkappa_1 - {\barr{\varkappa}}_1) \iint_{\mathcal{S}(s)} {X_2}^2 \;dX_1 dX_2 \end{aligned}$$
From [eq:M,eq:piola2,eq:stress_a,eq:strain_a] we deduce the constitutive equation for the second component of the internal moments :
$$ \begin{aligned} M_2 &= \iint_{\mathcal{S}(s)} ({\boldsymbol{r}} \times {\boldsymbol{\sigma_n}}(X_1, X_2, s, t)) \cdot {\boldsymbol{d}}_2 \;dX_1 dX_2 \\[0.5em] &= \iint_{\mathcal{S}(s)} - X_1\sigma_{33} \;dX_1 dX_2 \\[0.5em] &= E (\varkappa_2 - {\barr{\varkappa}}_2) \iint_{\mathcal{S}(s)} {X_1}^2 \;dX_1 dX_2 \end{aligned}$$

Summary of the theory

Let’s summarize the assumptions and results of Kirchhoff’s theory of rods on which our discret beam model will be based on.

In the reference configuration the rod is described by its reference strains :
$${\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_i}}'= {\barr{{\boldsymbol{\varkappa}}}} \times {\barr{{\boldsymbol{d}}_i}}$$
In the actual configuration the rod is described by its strains and spin vector :


$$\begin{aligned} {1} {\boldsymbol{x}}' &= (1+\epsilon){\boldsymbol{t}} \\ {\boldsymbol{d}}'_i&= {{\boldsymbol{\varkappa}}} \times {\boldsymbol{d}}_i \\ \dot{{\boldsymbol{d}}_i}&= {{\boldsymbol{\omega}}} \times {\boldsymbol{d}}_i \end{aligned}$$

The rod is subjected to internal forces and moments :


$$\begin{aligned} {1} {\boldsymbol{F}} &= F_1{\boldsymbol{d}}_1 + F_2{\boldsymbol{d}}_2 + F_3{\boldsymbol{d}}_3 \\ {\boldsymbol{M}} &= M_1{\boldsymbol{d}}_1 + M_2{\boldsymbol{d}}_2 + M_3{\boldsymbol{d}}_3 \end{aligned}$$

The rod is subjected to external and body loads described as distributed forces and moments acting on the centerline – either given per unit length of the reference configuration (fs, ms) or per unit length of the actual configuration (fst, mst) – and given in the condensed form :


$$\begin{aligned} {1} {\boldsymbol{f}} &= {\boldsymbol{f}}_{s} + (1+\epsilon){\boldsymbol{f}}_{s_t} = f_1{\boldsymbol{d}}_1 + f_2{\boldsymbol{d}}_2 + f_3{\boldsymbol{d}}_3 \\ {\boldsymbol{m}} &= {\boldsymbol{m}}_{s} + (1+\epsilon){\boldsymbol{m}}_{s_t} = m_1{\boldsymbol{d}}_1 + m_2{\boldsymbol{d}}_2 + m_3{\boldsymbol{d}}_3 \end{aligned}$$

The internal axial force, the internal bending moments and the internal twisting moment are computed with the following constitutive equations :


$$\begin{aligned} {1} F_3 &= ES\epsilon \label{eq:constitutive_a} \\ M_1 &= EI_1(\varkappa_1 - {\barr{\varkappa}}_1) \label{eq:constitutive_b} \\ M_2 &= EI_2(\varkappa_2 - {\barr{\varkappa}}_2) \label{eq:constitutive_c} \\ M_3 &= GJ(\varkappa_3 - {\barr{\varkappa}}_3) \label{eq:constitutive_d} \end{aligned}$$

where S, I1, I2, J are respectively the area, the second moments of inertia and the torsional stiffness of the cross-section :


$$\begin{aligned} {2} &S &&= \iint_{\mathcal{S}(s)} dX_1 dX_2 \\ &I_1 &&= \iint_{\mathcal{S}(s)} {X_2}^2 \;dX_1 dX_2 \\ &I_2 &&= \iint_{\mathcal{S}(s)} {X_1}^2 \;dX_1 dX_2 \\ &J &&= \iint_{\mathcal{S}(s)} X_1 \left( \frac{\partial \varphi_s}{\partial {X_2}} + X_1 \right ) - X_2 \left( \frac{\partial \varphi_s}{\partial {X_1}} - X_2 \right ) \;dX_1 dX_2 \end{aligned}$$

and φs is the warping fonction of the cross-section that satisfies the differential system :


$$\begin{aligned} {2} 0 &= \frac{\partial^2 \varphi_s}{\partial {X_1}^2} + \frac{\partial^2 \varphi_s}{\partial {X_2}^2} &&\quad,\quad \forall (X_1,X_2)\in\mathcal{S}(s) \\[0.5em] 0 &= \frac{\partial f_s}{\partial {X_1}}\left(\frac{\partial \varphi_s}{\partial {X_1}} - X_2 \right) + \frac{\partial f_s}{\partial {X_2}}\left(\frac{\partial \varphi_s}{\partial {X_2}} + X_1 \right) &&\quad,\quad f_s(X_1,X_2) = 0 \end{aligned}$$

The dynamical equations for the motion of the rod are :


$$\begin{aligned} {1} \frac{\partial {\boldsymbol{F}}}{\partial s} + {\boldsymbol{f}} &= \rho S \ddot{{\boldsymbol{x}}} \\[0.5em] \frac{\partial {\boldsymbol{M}}}{\partial s} + \frac{\partial {\boldsymbol{x}}}{\partial s} \times {\boldsymbol{F}} + {\boldsymbol{m}} &= \rho I_1 {\boldsymbol{d}}_1 \times \ddot{{\boldsymbol{d}}_1} + \rho I_2 {\boldsymbol{d}}_2 \times \ddot{{\boldsymbol{d}}_2} \end{aligned}$$

Neglecting the rotational dynamics around d1 and d2 the components of the above equations are written :

[eq:motion]
$$\begin{aligned} {1} F'_1 + \varkappa_2 F_3 - \varkappa_3 F_2 + f_1 &= \rho S \ddot{x}_1 \label{eq:motion_1} \\ F'_2 + \varkappa_3 F_1 - \varkappa_1 F_3 + f_2 &= \rho S \ddot{x}_2 \label{eq:motion_2} \\ F'_3 + \varkappa_1 F_2 - \varkappa_2 F_1 + f_3 &= \rho S \ddot{x}_3 \label{eq:motion_3} \\ M'_1 + \varkappa_2 M_3 - \varkappa_3 M_2 - (1+\epsilon)F_2 + m_1 & \simeq 0 \label{eq:motion_4} \\ M'_2 + \varkappa_3 M_1 - \varkappa_1 M_3 + (1+\epsilon)F_1 + m_2 & \simeq 0 \label{eq:motion_5} \\ M'_3 + \varkappa_1 M_2 - \varkappa_2 M_1 + m_3 & \simeq \rho (I_1 + I_2)\dot{\omega}_3 \label{eq:motion_6} \end{aligned}$$

The local displacements of the cross-sections are given by :


$$\begin{aligned} {1} u_1 &= -\nu \epsilon X_1 - \nu(\varkappa_1 - {\barr{\varkappa}}_1) X_1 X_2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\nu(\varkappa_2 - {\barr{\varkappa}}_2)({X_1}^2 - {X_2}^2) \\[0.5em] u_2 &= -\nu \epsilon X_2 + \nu(\varkappa_2 - {\barr{\varkappa}}_2) X_1 X_2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\nu(\varkappa_1 - {\barr{\varkappa}}_1)({X_1}^2 - {X_2}^2) \\[0.5em] u_3 &= (\varkappa_3 - {\barr{\varkappa}}_3)\varphi_s(X_1,X_2) \end{aligned}$$

The non-zero components of the strain tensor are given by :


$$\begin{aligned} {1} \epsilon_{33} &= \epsilon + (\varkappa_1 - {\barr{\varkappa}}_1) X_2 - (\varkappa_2 - {\barr{\varkappa}}_2) X_1 \\ \epsilon_{31} &= \tfrac{1}{2}(\varkappa_3 - {\barr{\varkappa}}_3)\left(\frac{\partial \varphi_s}{\partial {X_1}} - X_2 \right) \\ \epsilon_{32} &= \tfrac{1}{2}(\varkappa_3 - {\barr{\varkappa}}_3)\left(\frac{\partial \varphi_s}{\partial {X_2}} + X_1 \right) \\ \epsilon_{11} &= \epsilon_{22} = -\nu \epsilon_{33} \end{aligned}$$

The non-zero components of the stress tensor are given by :


$$\begin{aligned} {1} \sigma_{33} &= E \epsilon_{33} \\ \sigma_{31} &= 2G \epsilon_{31} \\ \sigma_{32} &= 2G \epsilon_{32} \end{aligned}$$

Geometric interpretation of Kirchhoff’s equations

The previous equations for the motion of the rod (see [eq:motion_1,eq:motion_2,eq:motion_3,eq:motion_4,eq:motion_5,eq:motion_6]) have been established expressing the fundamental principles of balance of linear and angular momentums. An alternative approach, leading to the same results, consists in differentiating the elastic energy of a given configuration of the rod – assumed to be stationary – with respect to the degrees of freedom of the mechanical system (principle of virtual work).44 However, the approach through equilibrium seems easier to understood as it is (almost) just a matter of balance between forces and moments on infinitesimal slices of the rods (see [fig:slice]). This is of obvious pedagogical interest as it allows to understand how the geometry of the rod influence the distribution of the elastic energy between extension, flexion and torsion. To emphasis this, we provide the proper drawings (see [fig:kb,fig:d1,fig:d2]) and computations for the contribution of internal forces and moments to the balance of linear and angular momentums. This is what we call the of Kirchhoff’s equations.

Flexion and torsion of an elementary slice of a Kirchhoff rod of length ds. Projections of the deformations are given in the material planes defined by d1, d2, d3.


Contributions to the balance of forces

N(s + ds) is deflected from d3(s) by the rotation of angle κds around κb ([fig:kb_b]). Thus, its contribution to the balance of forces onto d3(s) is :
N(s + ds)cos(κds)−N(s)=N′(s)ds + o(ds)

Contributions to the balance of moments

Q(s + ds) is deflected from d3(s) by the rotation of angle κds around κb ([fig:kb_c]). Thus, its contribution to the balance of moments onto d3(s) is :
Q(s + ds)cos(κds)−Q(s)=Q′(s)ds + o(ds)


Contributions to the balance of forces

T2(s + ds) is deflected from d2(s) by the combined rotations of angle τds around d3 and κ2ds around d2 ([fig:d1_b]). Thus, its contribution to the balance of forces onto d1(s) is :
−T2(s + ds)sin(τds)cos(κ2ds)= − τT2(s)ds + o(ds)

T2(s + ds) is deflected from d2(s) by the combined rotations of angle τds around d3 and κ1ds around d1 ([fig:d1_b]). Thus, its contribution to the balance of forces onto d2(s) is :
−T2(s)+T2(s + ds)cos(τds)cos(κ1ds)=T′2(s)ds + o(ds)

T2(s + ds) is deflected from d2(s) by the combined rotations of angle τds around d3 and κ1ds around d1 ([fig:d1_b]). Thus, its contribution to the balance of forces onto d3(s) is :
T2(s + ds)cos(τds)sin(κ1ds)=κ1T2(s)ds + o(ds)

N(s + ds) is deflected from d3(s) by the combined rotations of angle κ2ds around d2 and κ1ds around d1 ([fig:d1_b]). Thus, its contribution to the balance of forces onto d2(s) is :
−N(s + ds)cos(κ2ds)sin(κ1ds)= − κ1N(s)ds + o(ds)

Contributions to the balance of moments

T2(s + ds) is deflected from the plane normal to d1(s) by a rotation of angle τds around d3 ([fig:d1_b]). It produces a moment around d1 with the lever arm b = cos(κ2ds)ds. Thus, its contribution to the balance of moments onto d1(s) is :
−T2(s + ds)cos(τds)(cos(κ2ds)ds)= − T2(s)ds + o(ds)

M2(s + ds) is deflected from d2(s) by the combined rotations of angle τds around d3 and κ2ds around d2 ([fig:d1_c]). Thus, its contribution to the balance of moments onto d1(s) is :
−M2(s + ds)sin(τds)cos(κ2ds)= − τM2(s)ds + o(ds)

M2(s + ds) is deflected from d2(s) by the combined rotations of angle τds around d3 and κ1ds around d1 ([fig:d1_c]). Thus, its contribution to the balance of moments onto d2(s) is :
−M2(s)+M2(s + ds)cos(τds)cos(κ1ds)=M′2(s)ds + o(ds)

M2(s + ds) is deflected from d2(s) by the combined rotations of angle τds around d3 and κ1ds around d1 ([fig:d1_c]). Thus, its contribution to the balance of moments onto d3(s) is :
M2(s + ds)cos(τds)sin(κ1ds)=κ1M2(s)ds + o(ds)

Q(s + ds) is deflected from d3(s) by the combined rotations of angle κ2ds around d2 and κ1ds around d1 ([fig:d1_c]). Thus, its contribution to the balance of moments onto d2(s) is :
−Q(s + ds)cos(κ2ds)sin(κ1ds)= − κ1Q(s)ds + o(ds)


Contributions to the balance of forces

T1(s + ds) is deflected from d1(s) by the combined rotations of angle τds around d3 and κ2ds around d2 ([fig:d2_b]). Thus, its contribution to the balance of forces onto d1(s) is :
−T1(s)+T1(s + ds)cos(τds)cos(κ2ds)=T′1(s)ds + o(ds)

T1(s + ds) is deflected from d1(s) by the combined rotations of angle τds around d3 and κ1ds around d1 ([fig:d2_b]). Thus, its contribution to the balance of forces onto d2(s) is :
T1(s + ds)sin(τds)cos(κ1ds)=τT1(s)ds + o(ds)

T1(s + ds) is deflected from d1(s) by the combined rotations of angle τds around d3 and κ2ds around d2 ([fig:d2_b]). Thus, its contribution to the balance of forces onto d3(s) is :
−T1(s + ds)cos(τds)sin(κ2ds)= − κ2T1(s)ds + o(ds)

N(s + ds) is deflected from d3(s) by the combined rotations of angle κ1ds around d1 and κ2ds around d2 ([fig:d2_b]). Thus, its contribution to the balance of forces onto d1(s) is :
N(s + ds)cos(κ1ds)sin(κ2ds)=κ2N(s)ds + o(ds)

Contributions to the balance of moments

T1(s + ds) is deflected from the plane normal to d2(s) by the angle τds around d3 along ds ([fig:d2_b]). It produces a moment around d2 with the lever arm b = cos(κ1ds)ds. Thus, its contribution to the balance of moments onto d2(s) is :
T1(s + ds)cos(τds)(cos(κ1ds)ds)=T1(s)ds + o(ds)

M1(s + ds) is deflected from d1(s) by the combined rotations of angle τds around d3 and κ2ds around d2 ([fig:d2_c]). Thus, its contribution to the balance of moments onto d1(s) is :
−M1(s)+M1(s + ds)cos(τds)cos(κ2ds)=M′1(s)ds + o(ds)

M1(s + ds) is deflected from d1(s) by the combined rotations of angle τds around d3 and κ2ds around d2 ([fig:d2_c]). Thus, its contribution to the balance of moments onto d2(s) is :
M1(s + ds)sin(τds)cos(κ2ds)=τM1(s)ds + o(ds)

M1(s + ds) is deflected from d1(s) by the combined rotations of angle τds around d3 and κ2ds around d2 ([fig:d2_c]). Thus, its contribution to the balance of moments onto d3(s) is :
−M1(s + ds)cos(τds)sin(κ2ds)= − κ2M1(s)ds + o(ds)

Q(s + ds) is deflected from d3(s) by the combined rotations of angle κ1ds around d1 and κ2ds around d2 ([fig:d2_c]). Thus, its contribution to the balance of moments onto d1(s) is :
Q(s + ds)cos(κ1ds)sin(κ2ds)=κ2Q(s)ds + o(ds)

Conclusion

Remind that the beam is subject to a distributed external force fext and a distributed external moment mext.

F⊥(s)=d3 × (M′+𝜘 × M + mext)

F∥(s)=Nd3

F(s)=N(s)+T(s)
